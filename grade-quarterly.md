# Quarterly Post Grading Prompt

Paste this prompt into Claude (or equivalent) followed by the post content.

---

You are evaluating a quarterly update written by a software engineer for a personal site. A quarterly is not a performance review. It is not a LinkedIn post. It is an honest accounting of three months — what was worked on, what shipped, what didn't, what was learned, where things are going. The reader should finish it trusting the author more than they did before, because honesty about failure and uncertainty is rarer and more valuable than a curated highlight reel.

The target reader is a senior engineer, technical founder, or a peer early in their career looking for honest signal about what doing this kind of work actually looks like.

Grade the post on the following. For each criterion, give a pass, flag, or fail with one sentence of reasoning.

**1. Specificity of what was worked on**
Does it name actual projects, decisions, or outputs? Or does it describe activity ("spent time on X", "focused on Y") without saying what resulted? The reader should be able to reconstruct roughly what the author did, not just that they were busy.

**2. Honest about what didn't work or wasn't finished**
Does it mention anything that failed, stalled, or got cut? A quarter with no setbacks is either a lie or a boring quarter. Omitting friction makes the whole thing untrustworthy.

**3. No performed enthusiasm**
Flag any: "excited to share", "thrilled to announce", "incredibly grateful", or any language that sounds like a press release. Also flag: vague positive framing that obscures what actually happened ("made good progress on" without saying what progress means).

**4. What's next is honest, not aspirational**
Does the "what's next" section describe what will actually happen, or what the author wishes would happen? Aspirational forecasting is easy to write and useless to read. Flag any goal that has no constraint or tradeoff attached to it.

**5. Would the author send this to someone they respect and not wince?**
The test for a quarterly is whether a senior peer would read it and think "this person has good judgment and self-awareness" — not "this person is impressive." If it's optimized for impressiveness, it's failed at its actual job.

**6. Does it say something the reader couldn't have inferred from a resume?**
A quarterly should contain information that only exists because the author was honest. If everything in it could be extracted from a LinkedIn update, cut it and start over.

After grading each criterion, give an overall verdict: **publish**, **revise**, or **cut**. If revise, name the one thing that, if fixed, would make the rest worth keeping.
